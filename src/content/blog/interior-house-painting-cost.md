**The short answer:** Professional interior house painting costs between **$3,500 and $9,000** for a typical 1,500 to 2,000 sq ft home in 2026. On a per-room basis, expect to pay **$300 to $800 for a standard bedroom**, **$600 to $2,000 for a living room**, and **$150 to $400 for a bathroom**. Labor makes up roughly 80 to 85 percent of the total bill, so the region where you live and the quality of painter you hire matter far more than the paint brand.

Painting is one of the highest-return home improvements you can make. A fresh coat of interior paint can add $5,000 to $10,000 in perceived value before a sale, and it completely transforms how a space feels without the disruption of a major renovation. But the quotes you get from contractors can vary wildly. This guide will help you understand exactly why, so you do not overpay and you do not hire someone who will cut corners. If you are tackling multiple projects, you might also want to check our cost guides for [roof replacement](/blog/roof-replacement-cost/) and [water heater replacement](/blog/water-heater-replacement-cost/).

## Average Interior Painting Costs by Room

These estimates include labor, primer, and two coats of mid-grade paint on walls only. Trim, ceilings, and doors are extra and are broken out below.

| Room | Size (approx.) | Low Estimate | High Estimate | Average |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Small bedroom | 10x10 ft | $200 | $600 | $400 |
| Standard bedroom | 12x12 ft | $300 | $800 | $550 |
| Master bedroom | 14x16 ft | $500 | $1,200 | $800 |
| Bathroom | 6x8 ft | $150 | $400 | $275 |
| Kitchen | 10x12 ft | $350 | $900 | $600 |
| Living room | 15x20 ft | $600 | $2,000 | $1,200 |
| Dining room | 12x14 ft | $400 | $1,000 | $700 |
| Hallway / stairwell | varies | $250 | $800 | $500 |
| Full home (1,500 sq ft) | walls only | $3,500 | $7,500 | $5,200 |
| Full home (2,000 sq ft) | walls only | $4,500 | $9,000 | $6,500 |

**Important note on "square footage":** Painters typically charge by the square footage of paintable wall surface, not the floor area. A 12x12 bedroom with 8-foot ceilings has roughly 384 square feet of wall space (minus doors and windows). When you get quotes, ask whether the number refers to floor square footage or wall surface area, because the difference can throw off your comparison.

## Cost Per Square Foot: What Painters Actually Charge

On a per-square-foot basis (wall surface, not floor area):

- **Budget painters:** $1.50 to $2.50 per sq ft
- **Mid-range painters:** $2.50 to $4.00 per sq ft
- **Premium painters / high-end finishes:** $4.00 to $6.00 per sq ft

These ranges are for walls only. Ceilings typically add $0.75 to $1.50 per sq ft. Trim (baseboards, window casings, door frames) adds $1.00 to $3.00 per linear foot. If you have high or vaulted ceilings, tray ceilings, or detailed crown molding, expect to pay significantly more.

## What Drives Your Final Painting Bill

### 1. Labor: The Biggest Factor

Labor is 80 to 85 percent of the total cost of an interior paint job. A skilled painter in San Francisco or New York City will charge $50 to $80 per hour. The same quality of painter in rural Ohio or Mississippi might charge $25 to $40 per hour. The paint itself, whether you buy a $25 gallon or a $65 gallon, is a smaller driver of the total bill than most homeowners expect.

### 2. Surface Prep

This is where a lot of contractors quietly cut corners. Proper prep work includes:

- Cleaning walls with TSP (trisodium phosphate) or a TSP substitute to remove grease, dust, and grime
- Sanding rough spots and filling nail holes, cracks, and dents with spackle or joint compound
- Priming bare spots, stain-prone areas, or any surface going from dark to light
- Taping off trim, outlets, and fixtures

Prep can add $0.50 to $1.50 per square foot to the job. When a contractor gives you a very low bid, ask specifically how much prep work is included. Skipping prep is how you end up with a paint job that peels within two years.

### 3. Number of Coats

Most professional paint jobs include one coat of primer (where needed) and two coats of finish paint. If you are going from a dark color to a light color, or painting over a stain, three coats may be needed. Each additional coat adds roughly $0.50 to $1.00 per square foot to the job.

### 4. Paint Quality

Professional painters typically supply their own paint and mark it up. Here is what the major grades actually cost at the paint store:

| Grade | Cost Per Gallon | Coverage | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Budget (store brand) | $15 to $25 | 350 to 400 sq ft | Low-traffic rentals, temporary use |
| Mid-range (Benjamin Moore Regal, Sherwin-Williams SuperPaint) | $30 to $50 | 350 to 400 sq ft | Most rooms |
| Premium (Farrow and Ball, Benjamin Moore Aura) | $60 to $100 | 350 to 400 sq ft | High-traffic rooms, lasting quality |

For a room that you plan to live in for years, mid-range paint is usually the sweet spot. Premium paints do apply more smoothly and tend to hold color better, but the difference is not always visible once the paint dries. The [Consumer Reports paint ratings](https://www.consumerreports.org) are a good source if you want to compare specific products.

### 5. Ceiling Height

Standard 8-foot ceilings are priced into most standard quotes. If your home has:

- 9-foot ceilings: add roughly 10 to 15 percent
- 10-foot ceilings: add 15 to 25 percent
- Vaulted or cathedral ceilings: add 25 to 50 percent, plus possible scaffolding fees of $150 to $500

Stairwells are especially tricky because of the height and angle. Most painters charge $250 to $800 just for a stairwell, depending on how high it goes.

### 6. Finish Type

The sheen you choose affects both the look and the price:

- **Flat / Matte:** Hides imperfections well, common for ceilings and low-traffic areas
- **Eggshell:** A slight sheen, easy to clean, popular for living rooms and bedrooms
- **Satin:** More durable, good for hallways and high-traffic areas
- **Semi-gloss / Gloss:** Very washable, standard for trim, kitchens, and bathrooms

Most painters charge the same rate regardless of sheen. However, high-gloss finishes require more prep and more precise application, so a quality painter may charge slightly more.

## Regional Cost Differences Across the US

Where you live can shift your painting quote by 30 percent or more.

| Region | Adjustment vs. National Average |
|---|---|
| Northeast (NY, MA, CT, NJ) | +20 to +35% |
| West Coast (CA, WA, OR) | +15 to +30% |
| Mid-Atlantic (DC, MD, VA) | +10 to +20% |
| Mountain West (CO, UT, AZ) | +5 to +15% |
| Midwest (OH, IN, MI, MN) | -5 to +5% |
| South (TX, GA, FL, TN) | -10 to +5% |
| Rural areas nationwide | -15 to -25% |

A living room that costs $1,200 to paint in Cincinnati, Ohio might cost $1,800 in Boston and $2,000 in San Francisco. These differences reflect local labor rates, cost of living, and demand. The same quality of work underlies all of these quotes; you are not getting a better paint job in a higher-cost city.

## DIY vs. Hiring a Pro: An Honest Comparison

Painting is one of the more DIY-friendly home improvements. You do not need a license, and the tools are inexpensive. But "doable" and "worth it" are different questions.

| Factor | DIY | Hire a Pro |
|---|---|---|
| Cost for 1,500 sq ft home | $400 to $800 (paint plus supplies) | $3,500 to $7,500 |
| Time required | 3 to 7 full days | 2 to 4 days (not your time) |
| Quality | Depends heavily on prep and patience | Consistent if you vet properly |
| Equipment needed | Rollers, brushes, tape, drop cloths, ladder | Provided by contractor |
| Risk of mistakes | Drips, missed spots, uneven coverage common | Covered by warranty |
| Best for | Single rooms, rental-grade finish | Whole homes, high ceilings, premium finish |

If you are painting one room with straightforward walls and standard ceilings, DIY is a genuine money-saver. For a whole house, the time investment alone makes hiring a professional worthwhile for most people. A professional crew of two or three painters can finish your entire home in two to three days. That same job would take most homeowners two to three weekends and likely produce a noticeably less polished result.

## Getting Quotes: What to Ask Before You Sign Anything

Getting three quotes is the baseline. But asking the right questions is what separates a good hire from an expensive mistake.

**Ask every contractor:**

1. Is your company licensed and insured in this state? (Ask for the certificate of insurance, not just a verbal yes)
2. What prep work is included? Will you patch holes and sand before painting?
3. How many coats are included? Is primer included?
4. What brand and grade of paint will you use?
5. Who will do the actual painting? (Some contractors subcontract the labor)
6. What is the payment schedule? (Never pay more than 30 percent upfront)
7. Do you offer a warranty on your work?
8. How do you handle touch-ups if I am not satisfied?

A reputable painter will answer all of these questions without hesitation. Vague answers about prep work or paint grade are yellow flags. Pressure to pay a large deposit upfront is a red flag. For a complete contractor vetting checklist that applies to painters and every other home service, read our guide on [how to find a good contractor](/blog/how-to-find-good-contractor/).

You can verify contractor licenses through your state's contractor licensing board. The [National Association of Home Builders](https://www.nahb.org) maintains resources for finding credentialed professionals, and many states have their own licensing lookup tools.

## Red Flags That a Painter Will Cut Corners

It is worth knowing these before you sign a contract:

- **Unusually low bid:** If one quote is 40 percent below the other two, ask why. Often it means less prep, cheaper paint, or fewer coats.
- **No written contract:** Verbal agreements are not enforceable. Get everything in writing, including the paint brand and color, the number of coats, the prep steps, and the cleanup plan.
- **Cash-only and no paper trail:** This often means the contractor is not properly insured. If a worker is injured in your home and the contractor has no workers' compensation coverage, you can be held liable.
- **Demanding full payment upfront:** A standard payment schedule is 30 percent at signing, 30 percent at the midpoint, and 40 percent at completion.
- **No walk-through at the end:** A professional painter should walk through the finished job with you, address any touch-ups, and make sure you are satisfied before collecting final payment.

## How to Save Money Without Sacrificing Quality

You can meaningfully reduce your painting bill without sacrificing the result:

- **Do your own prep work.** Moving furniture, patching small nail holes, and cleaning walls yourself can cut $200 to $500 off a whole-home job.
- **Buy your own paint.** Some painters allow you to supply the paint. Buying directly from a paint store during a sale can save $15 to $25 per gallon.
- **Paint in the off-season.** Late fall through early spring is typically slower for painters. Many offer 10 to 15 percent discounts in January and February.
- **Combine rooms into one project.** Painters charge a trip fee and setup cost for each project. Getting four rooms done at once almost always costs less per room than four separate jobs.
- **Skip accent walls.** A single accent wall in a different color adds roughly $100 to $200 per room because of the masking and extra coat.

## How Lead Paint Affects Your Project

If your home was built before 1978, there is a real chance it contains lead-based paint. Under [EPA regulations](https://www.epa.gov/lead/renovation-repair-and-painting-program), contractors working on pre-1978 homes must follow the Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) Rule, which requires EPA-certified workers and specific containment procedures.

Hiring an RRP-certified contractor for a pre-1978 home typically adds 10 to 25 percent to the project cost. This is not optional: violations of the RRP rule carry fines up to $37,500 per day. More importantly, lead exposure is a serious health hazard, especially for children under 6. The [EPA's Lead Safe Certified Guide](https://www.epa.gov/lead) explains how to find certified contractors and what the procedures involve.

If you want to test your home for lead paint before starting any project, lead test kits are available at hardware stores for $10 to $30, and professional lead inspections cost $200 to $400.

## Painting and Indoor Air Quality

Most interior paints today are low-VOC or zero-VOC formulas, which is a genuine improvement over products from 20 years ago. VOCs (volatile organic compounds) are the chemicals that cause the "paint smell" and can trigger headaches, dizziness, and respiratory irritation.

The [EPA's indoor air quality guidance](https://www.epa.gov/indoor-air-quality-iaq) recommends:

- Ventilating rooms during and after painting by opening windows and running fans
- Using low-VOC or zero-VOC paint, especially in bedrooms, nurseries, and spaces with limited airflow
- Avoiding sleeping in a freshly painted room for at least 24 to 48 hours

Most major paint brands (Benjamin Moore, Sherwin-Williams, Behr) now offer extensive low-VOC lines at no significant price premium. If you have family members with asthma or chemical sensitivities, ask your painter or the paint store specifically about zero-VOC options.

## What Is Included in a Full Interior Paint Job

To avoid surprises, here is what a complete interior painting quote typically does and does not include:

**Usually included:**
- Walls in the agreed rooms
- One coat of primer on bare or heavily patched surfaces
- Two finish coats
- Masking and drop cloths
- Cleanup and removal of painter's tape and plastic

**Usually not included (ask about these):**
- Ceilings (often quoted separately)
- Trim, baseboards, crown molding, and window casings
- Doors and door frames
- Closet interiors
- Cabinets
- Accent or specialty colors

When comparing quotes, make sure each quote covers the same scope. A quote that includes ceilings and trim is not directly comparable to one that covers walls only.
