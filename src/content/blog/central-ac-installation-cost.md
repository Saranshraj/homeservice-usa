Installing central air conditioning costs **$3,500 to $7,500** for most US homeowners in 2026, with the national average landing around **$5,000 to $5,500** for a system replacement in a home that already has ductwork. If your home needs new ductwork installed alongside the AC unit, plan for **$6,500 to $12,000** for the total project. Labor accounts for roughly 40 to 50 percent of the bill, and the unit size (measured in tons) plus the efficiency rating (SEER2) determine most of the rest. The good news: the Inflation Reduction Act gives you a **30 percent tax credit up to $600** on qualifying central AC units, and heat pump systems qualify for up to **$2,000**.

## What You Will Pay: A Quick Summary

Before we get into details, here is a snapshot of the most common scenarios:

| Scenario | Typical Cost |
|---|---|
| AC replacement, existing ductwork | $3,500 to $7,500 |
| New AC installation, new ductwork | $6,500 to $12,000 |
| Mini-split (ductless), single zone | $2,500 to $5,500 |
| Mini-split, multi-zone (3 to 4 zones) | $7,000 to $15,000 |
| Window unit (per room, DIY) | $150 to $700 |

If your current AC is still working but over 15 years old and you live in a hot climate, a replacement will almost always pay for itself in energy savings within 7 to 10 years, especially with today's high-efficiency SEER2 systems.

## Cost by System Size

Air conditioners are sized in tons of cooling capacity. One ton equals 12,000 BTUs per hour. A correctly sized system is critical: too small and it runs nonstop without cooling your home; too large and it short-cycles, leaving your home humid and muggy.

A general rule of thumb: one ton covers roughly 400 to 600 square feet, though climate, insulation quality, ceiling height, and window count all affect the calculation. Your HVAC contractor should perform a Manual J load calculation to size the system properly.

| System Size | Typical Home Size | Installed Cost (no ductwork work) |
|---|---|---|
| 1.5 ton | 600 to 900 sq ft | $2,800 to $4,500 |
| 2 ton | 900 to 1,200 sq ft | $3,200 to $5,200 |
| 2.5 ton | 1,200 to 1,500 sq ft | $3,600 to $5,800 |
| 3 ton | 1,500 to 2,000 sq ft | $4,000 to $6,500 |
| 3.5 ton | 2,000 to 2,400 sq ft | $4,500 to $7,000 |
| 4 ton | 2,400 to 3,000 sq ft | $5,000 to $7,500 |
| 5 ton | 3,000 to 4,000 sq ft | $5,500 to $9,000 |

These prices include the condensing unit (the outdoor box), the air handler or evaporator coil, refrigerant, and labor. They assume existing ductwork in good condition.

## What Drives the Cost Up (and Down)

### SEER2 Rating

The Department of Energy updated its efficiency standard in January 2023, replacing the old SEER rating with SEER2. The minimum allowed efficiency now depends on where you live:

- **Northern states** (most of the US): 13.4 SEER2 minimum
- **Southern and Southwestern states** (FL, TX, AZ, GA, NV, and others): 15.2 SEER2 minimum

A minimum-code unit at 14 to 16 SEER2 costs less upfront. A high-efficiency unit at 18 to 26 SEER2 costs $500 to $1,500 more but can cut your cooling bills by 20 to 40 percent. If you run your AC from May through October, the higher-efficiency unit often pays back the premium within 5 to 7 years. In the South, where AC runs 8 months a year, the math tips toward high efficiency even faster.

### Brand

You will see a wide range of prices depending on the brand your contractor recommends:

| Brand Tier | Examples | Price Premium vs. Average |
|---|---|---|
| Economy | Goodman, Payne | 15 to 25% below average |
| Mid-range | Rheem, Ruud, York | Near average |
| Premium | Carrier, Trane, Lennox | 20 to 40% above average |

Economy brands like Goodman are manufactured by the same parent company (Daikin) that owns more premium lines. The core technology is similar. The main difference is warranty coverage, features, and the reputation that comes with a familiar name. For most homeowners, a mid-range brand with a 10-year parts warranty is a solid choice.

### Labor and Regional Differences

HVAC labor rates vary significantly by region:

| Region | Typical Labor Rate (per hour) |
|---|---|
| Southeast and Midwest | $65 to $100 |
| Mid-Atlantic and Texas | $85 to $125 |
| Northeast and California | $100 to $175 |

Labor typically takes 4 to 8 hours for a straightforward replacement. A first-time installation with ductwork can take 2 to 4 days.

### Ductwork

This is where budgets can balloon. If your home has no existing ductwork (common in older homes built with boilers or radiators), adding a full duct system costs:

- **Partial ductwork repairs or modifications:** $500 to $2,500
- **Complete new ductwork for a medium home (1,500 to 2,500 sq ft):** $3,000 to $7,000

If your existing ducts are dirty, leaking, or undersized, a duct sealing service costs $400 to $1,500 and can improve system efficiency by 20 to 30 percent. The Department of Energy estimates that duct leakage causes the average home to lose 25 to 30 percent of conditioned air before it reaches the living space.

## The 2026 Refrigerant Transition: What It Means for Your Wallet

If you have been getting quotes and hearing contractors mention refrigerant, here is important context for 2026.

R-410A, the refrigerant that has been used in virtually all central AC systems for the past two decades, is being phased down under the American Innovation and Manufacturing (AIM) Act due to its high global warming potential. Production was significantly curtailed starting January 1, 2025.

What this means for you:

- **Repairing older R-22 systems:** R-22 (Freon) was phased out in 2020 and is now extremely expensive, often $100 to $175 per pound. If your pre-2010 system needs a refrigerant recharge, it is almost always cheaper to replace the whole unit.
- **R-410A systems built before 2025:** These systems will still be serviced, but R-410A prices are rising. A small leak repair that cost $150 two years ago may cost $250 to $400 today.
- **New systems:** New units sold in 2026 use next-generation refrigerants like R-32 or R-454B, which have a much lower environmental impact and are covered under warranty. This is actually good news: new systems are quieter, more efficient, and use refrigerants that will remain available for decades.

The bottom line is that if your AC is more than 12 to 15 years old and needs a refrigerant recharge or compressor repair, the math almost always favors replacement over repair in 2026.

## Heat Pumps: A Smarter Option in 2026?

A heat pump does what a central AC does in summer, but also provides heating in winter. In moderate climates (most of the US except extreme northern regions), a heat pump can replace both your AC and furnace.

The installed cost is similar to a traditional AC: **$4,000 to $8,500** for most homes. But the IRS tax credit is significantly better. Under the Inflation Reduction Act:

- **Central AC (qualifying):** 30 percent of cost, up to **$600** per year (IRS Form 5695)
- **Heat pump:** 30 percent of cost, up to **$2,000** per year (IRS Form 5695)

If you are replacing an aging furnace and AC at the same time, a heat pump may cost less in total than replacing both systems separately, and you get a larger tax credit to boot. Your state may also offer additional rebates. The ENERGY STAR website has a rebate finder tool that shows what is available in your zip code.

## How to Get an Accurate Quote (and Not Overpay)

Here is the single most important thing to know: HVAC pricing varies wildly between contractors, sometimes by $2,000 to $3,000 for identical equipment. Getting three quotes is not just good practice; it can save you serious money.

When getting quotes, insist on the following:

1. **Manual J load calculation.** Any contractor who recommends a system size without measuring your home, checking insulation, and counting windows is guessing. An oversized system creates humidity problems and wears out faster.
2. **Itemized quote.** Ask for the equipment model number, SEER2 rating, warranty terms, and labor cost broken out separately. This lets you compare apples to apples.
3. **Permits.** In most states, AC installation requires a permit and inspection. Any contractor who offers to skip the permit is not just cutting corners on paperwork; an unpermitted system can create problems when you sell your home and may void your homeowner's insurance coverage.
4. **NATE certification.** North American Technician Excellence (NATE) is the industry's leading certification. Choosing a NATE-certified technician is not required, but it signals a higher level of verified competence.

Verify your contractor's license through your state licensing board before signing anything. In most states you can look this up in 60 seconds online. For a complete step-by-step vetting process, read our guide on [how to find a good contractor and avoid scams](/blog/how-to-find-good-contractor/).

## When to Repair vs. Replace

Not every AC problem requires a full replacement. Here is a simple framework:

| Situation | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| System is under 10 years old, needs minor repair | Repair |
| System is 10 to 15 years old, repair cost over $1,000 | Consider replacement |
| System is over 15 years old, any significant repair | Replace |
| Refrigerant leak in an R-22 system | Replace immediately |
| System running but struggling to cool adequately | Get efficiency analysis; likely replacement |

If your AC is still working but blowing warm air, check our [AC troubleshooting guide](/blog/ac-blowing-warm-air/) before spending on a full replacement. A useful rule of thumb: if the repair cost exceeds 50 percent of the cost of a new system, replacement is the smarter financial move. A new system also comes with a fresh warranty, better efficiency, and a refrigerant that will be supported for decades.

## Energy Savings: What to Expect

Upgrading from an old 10 SEER system (which was minimum code before 2006) to a modern 18 SEER2 unit can cut your cooling costs by 35 to 45 percent. According to the Department of Energy, air conditioning accounts for about 12 percent of total home energy costs nationally, but in hot southern states it can be 25 to 50 percent of the summer electric bill.

For a home spending $200 per month on summer cooling with an old unit, upgrading to a high-efficiency system could save $70 to $90 per month from June through September, or roughly $300 to $360 per summer. Over 15 years, that is $4,500 to $5,400 in savings.
